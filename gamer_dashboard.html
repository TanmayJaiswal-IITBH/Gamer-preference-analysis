The Big Picture
Casual Gaming is Mostly Harmless — Until It Isn't

For most students, gaming is a neutral leisure activity with no academic damage. But a clear tipping point emerges around 4 hours per day — where addiction, sleep disruption, and academic risk spike together.

Core Finding: 54% of respondents report zero academic impact from gaming. But for heavy gamers (4+ hrs/day), average addiction scores jump to 4.33/5 and sleep quality collapses — revealing a stark dose-response relationship that Logistic Regression confirms with 81.3% accuracy.
🎮
Entertainment Dominates — 59.2%
Nearly 3 in 5 gamers play purely for entertainment. Stress relief accounts for another 31.6%, making casual leisure the overwhelming motivation.
⏰
Late Night is the Default Window
58% of all gamers play between 9 PM and 2 AM — confirming gaming as an end-of-day wind-down ritual, not daytime procrastination.
📉
4+ Hours = Tipping Point
Players at 4+ hrs/day score 4.33 addiction on average, vs 2.12 for <1 hr. Sleep scores also collapse from median 3 to near 5 (worst).
🤝
Social Gamers Make Online Friends
Players who game with real-life friends are ~4× more likely to also form online friendships — social behavior spills from physical to digital.
📱
Mobile & PC Dominate
Mobile (59 users) and PC (39 users) dwarf console gaming. Low cost barriers for college students make these the default platforms.
🧪
Stress Relief Paradox
Gaming for entertainment reduces stress reliably. But players who deliberately use gaming to cope show scattered, higher stress scores — the coping strategy backfires.

Statistical Relationships
Hours Drive Addiction. Addiction Drives Everything Else.

The correlation matrix reveals a clear causal chain: more hours → higher addiction → worse sleep → worse academics. Stress, however, is decoupled — addicted players aren't necessarily more stressed.

Key Correlation Insights
Hours ↔ Addiction: 0.49 — Strong positive link. More playtime reliably predicts higher self-reported addiction scores.
Addiction ↔ Sleep: 0.30 — Moderate positive. Addicted players report worse sleep quality.
Hours ↔ Sleep: ~0.00 — Surprising zero correlation. Hours alone don't hurt sleep — only when addiction is also present.
Stress ↔ Addiction: −0.09 — Near zero. Being addicted doesn't make someone more or less stressed — stress is independent.
Stress ↔ Sleep: 0.01 — No relationship. These are genuinely orthogonal dimensions of gaming impact.
